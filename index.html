Project Overview

What We Know — and What We Don't

Scientists know that plastics and the petrochemicals in them have climate change impacts. But we don't know how much of temperature rise they may cause. With increasing plastic production, use, and waste generation, these climate impacts will only grow.

Even many "bio" and "compostable" plastics affect the climate, but to what extent remains unclear. Critically, a number of climate impacts from plastics are not included in any greenhouse gas emissions inventories, climate models, or scenarios — including those published by the IPCC.

The goal of The Plastics & Climate Project is to help estimate the extent to which plastics and their associated petrochemicals contribute to global average temperature rise.

We have conducted a systematic review of peer-reviewed scientific literature to identify critical data gaps, developed an agenda for necessary continued research, and recommended policies and actions to support that research — and to ensure plastics' climate impacts are included in relevant analyses.

Our findings are shared through a peer-reviewed paper published in Frontiers in Environmental Science and a summary report for non-technical audiences. We aim to generate support for the research and inclusion of plastic climate impacts in modeling, accounting, reporting, scenarios, and policies worldwide.

Project Rationale

Three Ways Plastics Drive Climate Change

Though the connections between climate change and plastics are becoming more widely discussed, little quantitative data exists. As fossil fuels are the main feedstock for 99% of plastics, the industry inherently has climate impacts — and several unique ones too.

01

Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Emissions of multiple greenhouse gases and black carbon throughout the lifecycle of plastics — from extraction and production through distribution, use, end-of-life treatment, and unmanaged waste.

02

Carbon Cycle Interference

Interference with global carbon cycling, including the functioning of ocean, soil, and plant carbon sinks that the planet depends on to absorb CO₂.

03

Radiation Budget Alteration

Alteration of the Earth's radiation budget due to plastic particles covering the Earth's surface and their presence in the air and clouds.

Key Facts

If the plastics industry were a country, it would be at least the 5th largest emitter of greenhouse gases — after China, the US, India, and Russia.

The plastics industry is on track to release more greenhouse gases than US coal plants by 2030, including millions of tons of methane.

Plastics appear to be harming the ocean's ability to absorb carbon, as well as soils' and plants', while adding toxins to those environments and the air we breathe.

Plastics and petrochemicals are harming human health, especially for frontline and environmental justice communities near fossil fuel extraction and plastics facilities.

Project Deliverables

What the Project Has Produced

From rigorous scientific review to public advocacy, The Plastics & Climate Project spans research, policy, and communication.

🔬

Systematic Literature Review

Meta-analysis assessing the quantitative relationship between plastics and climate change across ~6,400 peer-reviewed papers.

📋

Research Agenda

Proposed experiments and studies needed to address the data and knowledge gaps identified through the review.

📄

Peer-Reviewed Paper

Published in Frontiers in Environmental Science, April 2025, sharing findings with the global scientific community.

📰

Summary Report

A non-technical report summarizing findings and recommendations for policymakers, educators, and advocates.

🎙️

Symposia & Webinars

Events connecting leading scientists, policymakers, NGOs, and EJ representatives to present project findings.

🌐

Resource Repository

An ongoing public repository of papers, reports, articles, and recordings on the plastics/climate nexus.
